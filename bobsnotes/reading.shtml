For our text we are reading the Megiddo from Sethe's text covering the first independent campaign launched into Retenu by King Djehytmes III. The reading assignments are contained within the document.

A transcription by Brugsch will also serve as a text, but it lacks reading assignments. You can translate either, (or your own), just let us know which text you are reading and maintain correlatation between your text and the reading assignments from the Sethe text above.

Sethe's text contains notes in German translated by me (reasonably well, I think) in a separate document located here , and will be updated as the course continues. The latest revision date is 2014 April 15.

Also available are drawings by Lepsius covering most, but not all, of the material in the text, here , here , here, and here .

Maps are essential for an understanding of the text and the motivations of the principals. The first is the Campaign Overview using a base map from BYU Geography Department.

The Via Maris as it passes through modern Israel is shown in closer detail. This map emphasizes the strategic position of Megiddo and provides understanding of why this is one of the most fought- over spots in the world. It guards the entrance to the Plain of Esdraelon to the north. This entire region must be viewed, with few exceptions, as hostile to Djehutymes III while the few exceptions must be regarded with suspicion.

This map shows the situation on the chart table at the commanders' conference at Yehem. The site of Megiddo covers all approaches to Galilee to the north no matter which pass the Egyptians choose to force their way through.

A map of the immediate environs of Megiddo shows the exit from the Aruna Pass and the general location of the Egyptian camp. Highway 66 heads south toward Taanach. Note that by camping between the force at Taanach and Megiddo, Djehutymes would force the Taanach detachment, not by itself strong enough to defeat the Egyptian army, to make a wide circuit around to the east before being able to rejoin the main force.

Nelson's identifications of the topography in the immediate region of Megiddo are marked on this map for reference. Harold Hayden Nelson wrote his PhD. thesis on this fight and had boots on the ground in 1912, when the area looked much more like it did in Djehutymes III's time than it does now.
