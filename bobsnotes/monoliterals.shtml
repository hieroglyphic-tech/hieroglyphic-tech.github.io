MONOLITERALS

also called UNILITERALS

Monoliteral signs represent one consonant. All of the consonants in the language are represented in this list. Learn the signs, the transliteration of the sound, and the Manuel de Codage (MdC) equivalent of the tranliteration symbol. You can pay less attention to the descriptions of the signs and the English equivalents which are only guides to transcribing Egyptian names into English.

The order these signs appear in is the order which Egyptologists use to "alphabetize" their dictionaries, vocabularies, and word lists. Learn it. This sequence was established by early Egyptologists before modern studies of the sounds of speech were initiated. As a result, the terms used on this page are not technically accurate, but are used to conveniently organize the signs into useful categories.

The English letter version of MdC (Manual de Codage), which appears below, distinguishes carefully between upper and lower case letters.

All of these glyphs represented sounds which in Old Egyptian were consonants.
Their pronounciation in Middle Egyptian is controversial but in many cases had apparently been softened to the point of sounding like vowels.
Whatever G1 sounded like was particularly variable, particularly when used in spelling foreign words and names where it could be used as a consonant of varying quality.
Z4 could always be substituted for the doubled reed feather M17-M17.
Z7 likewise could always be substituted for the chick G43.

Sounds in this class are made mostly with the breath.
h is a normal "h" sound as usually found in English.
H is a more emphatic "h" sound, somewhat harsher to the ear.
x is the sound you make when clearing your throat, as "ch" in German "buch" or Scottish "loch".
X is like x except pronounced higher in the windstream and often approaches (but is never exactly alike) the sound of 'sh' in English, something like the sound of "ch" as "ich" is pronounced in some dialects of German.

The first glyph, O34, was originally a "z" sound as in English but by Middle Egyptian times it had become indistinguishable from S29, an "s" sound. As a result, both glyphs could be used interchangeably.
The last sign, N37, was probably a palatalized "s", close to, but not exactly, English "sh". At any rate "sh" can be used when pronouncing this sound.

Originally four distinct consonants in Old Egyptian, these merged into only two distinct sounds by Middle Egyptian times.
X1 and D46 were pronounced as in English.
V13, pronounced by Egyptologists like English "ch" as in "church", was probably a palatal "t", close to "ch", but not exaclty. It merged with X1 and the glyphs became interchangeable.
I10 is prounounced by Egyptologists like the English "j" as in "Juliet" but was probably a palatal "d", close to "j" but not exactly. It merged with D46 and the glyphs became interchangeable.
